# Presentation Skill

## When to use this skill
User wants to build a presentation — from scratch or rough input (topic, audience, key points). Produce: a **brief**, **slide-by-slide content**, and **speaker notes per slide**. Do not generate files or PowerPoint output.

---

## Step 1 — Clarify before building

Ask only what's missing:
- **Goal** — what should the audience *do or decide* after this?
- **Audience** — who are they, what do they know, what might they resist?
- **Format** — how long? how many slides? any hard constraints?
- **Input** — what must be included: data, stories, features, key messages?
- **Stage** — is this early concept, detailed design, or final pitch? (affects visual fidelity direction)

Do not proceed until goal and audience are clear.

---

## Step 2 — Dramaturgy

Before writing slides, define the dramatic structure:

**Goal**
"After this, the audience will ___."
Never: "Tell about our product." Always: "Convince the team to approve the Q3 roadmap."

**Character & Problem** *(for design presentations)*
Give the user a name, a role, a real goal. Their goal is never "use the product" — it's what the product helps them achieve. Test with "why?": why do they want this? That's the real goal.
Opening: a concrete story with date, place, people — or a striking statistic with a source. Never open with "Today I'll talk about..."

**The Enemies (3 max)**
The real objections *this* audience holds. Specific, not generic. The 3 strongest things standing between them and agreement. The whole presentation fights these.

**The Arguments**
For each enemy, one counter:
- Story — proves a single case, humanizes the problem
- Statistic — convinces skeptics, always cite the source
- Analogy — makes abstract concrete; one per presentation max

Use only the 3–5 strongest. Never dilute strong arguments with weak ones.

**Closing**
Return to the opening story. Show the resolution — how the character's life is better now. Issue one clear call to action (a decision, approval, next step). Never end on Q&A alone.

---

## Step 3 — Output

### A. Presentation Brief
- Stated goal (audience action)
- Character: name, role, real goal, core problem
- The 3 enemies and the argument mapped to each
- One allegory if applicable
- Slide outline: number → headline → content type (story / stat / visual / argument / CTA)

### B. Slide Content
For each slide:
- **Headline** — one sentence; the conclusion or hook, not a topic label
- **Body** — only what appears on the slide; minimal text
- **Visual direction** — type and purpose: photo (impress/prove), chart (prove with data), icon (remind/explain), diagram (explain/prove). Never decorative.

Slide rules (Kapterev):
- One focal point per slide
- At least 30% whitespace
- Concrete language only. Bad: "Rich user experience." Good: "Task completed in 2 steps instead of 7."
- Every element must justify its presence. Delete anything that doesn't move the story forward.

### C. Speaker Notes (per slide)
For each slide:
- What to say out loud — adds to the slide, never repeats it
- Which enemy this slide is fighting (if applicable)
- Interaction cue: when to pause, when to ask the audience a question
- Time estimate for this slide

---

## Quality checklist

- [ ] Opening does not start with "Today I will..." or a self-introduction
- [ ] Character has a name, a real goal, and a concrete problem
- [ ] Each of the 3 enemies is explicitly addressed and defeated
- [ ] No slide has more text than the audience can read in 5 seconds
- [ ] Every visual has a stated purpose — nothing decorative
- [ ] Speaker notes add information beyond what's on the slide
- [ ] Closing returns to the opening story and ends with a clear CTA
- [ ] Total arc: hook → problem → enemies defeated → resolution → CTA
